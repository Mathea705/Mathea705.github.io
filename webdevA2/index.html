The Fundamentals of Art

What is Color Theory?

Color theory is the study of how colors work together to create harmony and visual interest.

Artists use color theory to decide which colors look good together and how to set a certain mood in their work.

Primary Colors

These are the three basic colors that cannot be made by mixing others.

Here are some examples:

  • Red
  • Blue
  • Yellow

Secondary Colors

These colors are made by mixing two primary colors.

Here are some examples:

  • Green is made by mixing blue and yellow.
  • Orange is made by mixing red and yellow.
  • Purple is made by mixing red and blue.

Tertiary Colors

Tertiary colors are made by mixing a primary color with a nearby secondary color.

Here are some examples:

  • Red-Orange
  • Yellow-Green
  • Blue-Violet

Complementary Colors

These are colors that are directly opposite each other on the color wheel.

Here are some examples:

  • Red and Green
  • Blue and Orange
  • Yellow and Purple

When used together, they create strong contrast and vibrant visuals.

Analogous colors are next to each other on the color wheel and often match well.

They create a calm, pleasing feel when viewed.

Here are some examples:

  • Red, Orange and Yellow
  • Blue, Blue-Green and Green

Warm vs Cool Colors

  • Warm colors (like red, orange, yellow) make a scene feel energetic or intense.
  • Cool colors (like blue, green, purple) give a calm or sad feeling.

Artists use warm colors to show heat, excitement, or action, and cool colors to show peace or stillness.

What is Anatomy?

Anatomy is the study of the structure and form of the human body and how its parts work together.

Artists use anatomy to draw realistic figures, understand proportions, and create believable poses and movements in their artwork.

Proportions of the Human Body

Understanding proportions is essential in figure drawing. Classic guidelines suggest that an average adult is about 7 to 8 heads tall.

Knowing how long the arms, legs, and torso should be in relation to each other helps artists draw more realistic and balanced figures.

These rules also help identify and intentionally exaggerate features in stylized or cartoon art.

Muscle Groups and Structure

Muscles define the shape and movement of the human body.

Artists often focus on major muscle groups when sketching dynamic poses such as:

  • Deltoids (Shoulders)
  • Pectorals (Chest)
  • Biceps/Triceps (Arms)
  • Quadriceps (Legs)

Even in simplified drawings, understanding how muscles flex and stretch can add energy and realism to your characters.

Skeleton as the Foundation

The skeleton acts as the underlying framework for all human figures.

Learning how bones like the spine, ribcage, pelvis, and limbs connect helps with constructing believable poses.

Understanding proportions is essential in figure drawing. Classic guidelines suggest that an average adult is about 7 to 8 heads tall.

Gesture drawing often starts with basic bone structure before layering muscles and details on top.

Studying the skeleton also improves your ability to draw from imagination.

What is Perspective?

Perspective in art is the technique used to represent three-dimensional depth and space on a flat surface.

It helps artists create the illusion of distance, depth, and spatial relationships in their compositions.

1-Point Perspective

This is the simplest form of perspective, using a single vanishing point on the horizon line.

It is useful for drawing objects like roads, hallways, or buildings viewed straight on.

All lines converge to one point, creating depth.

2-Point Perspective

In this method, two vanishing points are placed on the horizon line.

This perspective is often used for drawing buildings from a corner view.

It allows for more dynamic and realistic depth compared to 1-point.

3-Point Perspective

This method uses three vanishing points, two on the horizon, and one either above or below.

It creates dramatic viewpoints, such as looking up at a tall skyscraper or down from a high place.

This is often used for extreme angles or fantasy architecture.

Atmospheric Perspective

Instead of vanishing points, this technique uses color, value, and contrast to show depth.

Farther objects appear lighter, blurrier, and cooler in color to simulate air or mist between viewer and object.

What is Composition?

Composition is how artists arrange visual elements within their artwork to create balance, focus, and visual flow.

Good composition guides the viewer's eye and creates a pleasing, harmonious image.

Rule of Thirds

The rule of thirds divides your canvas into a 3√ó3 grid.

Placing important elements along these lines or at their intersections creates more dynamic, interesting compositions.

This technique helps avoid placing subjects directly in the center, which can feel static.

Leading Lines

Leading lines guide the viewer's eye through your composition toward the main subject or focal point.

Different types of lines create different feelings:

  • Diagonal lines: Dynamic and energetic
  • Horizontal lines: Calm and peaceful
  • Vertical lines: Strong and powerful
  • Curved lines: Graceful and flowing

Visual Balance

Balance in composition refers to how visual weight is distributed across your artwork.

Symmetrical balance: Equal weight on both sides creates formal, stable feeling.

Asymmetrical balance: Unequal but balanced elements create dynamic, interesting compositions.

Factors affecting visual weight include size, color intensity, and position.

Creating Focal Points

A focal point is the main area of interest that draws the viewer's attention first.

You can create focal points through:

  • Contrast: Different colors, values, or textures
  • Size: Making one element larger than others
  • Isolation: Surrounding the subject with empty space
  • Convergence: Leading lines pointing to the subject

Good focal points prevent viewers from getting lost and guide them through your artwork.
